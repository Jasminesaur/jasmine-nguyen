ACCESSIBILITY RESEARCH

October 2015 - March 2016
Worked on accessibility research on the side under the Informatics PhD student Mark Baldwin.

THE BEGINNING The Problem: Individuals, who use a screen reader, tend to have difficulties using their system to effectively go through a web page.
A lot of screen readers tend to read unnecessary content that no one is interested in.
Individuals, that don't use a screen reader, tend to see what they want and they'll click it or type what they want into the search bar.
Individuals, that use a screen reader, don't have the same privilege.
This is important because, according to a 2015 article on webaim.org, 2307 respondents out of 2478 (over 93.1%) use a screen reader due to a disability.
When asked if web content has become more accessible, 38.7% said it has, 37.7% said it hasn't, and 23.6% said it's become less accessible.
Since it would be difficult to convince businesses to create more accessible web content, we need an efficient way to get the general populace to understand web accessibility.

It all started with Mark's discussion on accessibility. He began the focus on accessibility in technology with Twitter to start with.
I looked for sentiment using the tweet #GAAD.
Originally, we were focused on using R to find all of the tweets; however, "TwitteR" only offered tweets from the past few days.
With that, I looked through the entirety of Twitter to find tweets made on Global Accessibility Awareness Day.
Overall, people liked what GAAD stood for; however, others didn't know what it was.

After checking through Twitter, I looked at other websites that focused on GAAD.
I looked through Stack websites, Reddit, Imgur, Tumblr, Facebook, blogs, and articles made on different news sites.
Many websites didn't offer information on "GAAD". Instead, I found pictures and articles that did not contain actual sentiment from the user.
Once we hit a dead end with #GAAD, we used those websites to focus on developers in accessibility.

SPREADSHEETS I compiled a list of all the research data into a spreadsheet.
Accessibility Spreadsheets
I included a list of 62 startup companies and the amount of errors, alerts, features, structural elements, HTML 5 and ARIA, and contrast errors that their websites have.


On the side, I looked at Stack Overflow, people's blogs, Reddit, and ACM.
With that, I mentioned their relevance to accessibility and gave a quick summary.
I also included conversations from Hacker News that focused specifically on the topics and keywords of accessibility.
I searched for terms like: accessibility, a11y, 508, WAI, compliance, assistive technology, web accessibility, disability,
blind developer, GAAD, visually impaired, global accessibility, and awareness.


At the time, I was still trying to understand the role of accessibility in software development.
The goal was to uncover a problem that was not yet talked about and address it.

There were a few themes I was looking for:
1. How does accessibility factor into startup MVP's.
2. How are accessibility rules being misused or not used.
3. How has accessibility been implemented.
4. Mobile accessibility.

Through those themes, I focused on startup companies and how accessibility differs compared to established companies.
Overall, we evaluated 40-50 startup companies and pulled their data from CrunchBase.
2-5 companies were selected from that list and I evaluated their accessibility using WebAim.

COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS Then, I created a comparative analysis chart that showed the comparison of startup companies to the competing established companies.


I compared online educational sites, media sites, radio stations, news sites, food sites, and merchandise sites.
For the most part, the startup company will contain more accessibility errors than the established company.
However, there were a few surprises with the established site being less accessible than the startup site.
It was rare, but it was interesting to see.

THE APPLICATION Afterward, I compared 13 plugins and extensions that were similar to what we wanted to create: a plugin that helps with accessibility.

Our idea was to have a visually sighted person and a screen-reader person download the same plugin, and it would focus on crowdsourcing.
The plugin on the visually sighted side would collect all the user's clicks when it's on.
The plugin would have an algorithm that distinguished where most users clicked first.
On the screen-reader side, the plugin would first say the links that are the most clicked.
Subsequently, I created 15 interview questions that we were going to ask users when we completed the plugin.

A couple questions were:
1. What are your thoughts on this system?
2. Do you give permission for it to collect all your "clicks" and sites you visit?

We were afraid that people didn't want to download the plugin due to security reasons; however, we still believed it would be for the greater good.
We then completed around 30 paper prototypes of the design that varied from how the plugin would act when it was in incognito mode to how it would visually look.
Afterward, I then created 4 storyboards to showcase how the application would be used.





THE END After being done with the competitive analysis chart, I ended up finding an app that was almost identical to what we wanted to create.
The Blank App by Code the Dream.
We were extremely happy to see that an app has been created that served almost the same purpose.

The topic was very interesting, and I wonder why accessibility isn't more of a priority for startup companies.
If I was still working on this research, I would focus on one more thing:
If compliance is still a problem, could we rely on other tools to make up for the lack of compliance?

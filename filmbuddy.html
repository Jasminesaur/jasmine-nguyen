FILMBUDDY

February 20, 2015 - May 11, 2015
FilmBuddy is an application created in INF 131: Human Computer Interaction by a group of 6 students with one idea: to create an accessible movie theater app.


STARTING OUT The beginning of the project was rather simple. Before being set into teams, we were told to choose an area and observe it.
Without being told to observe anything specific, I began by observing the movie theater.

PROBLEM AREA: I decided on the movie theater because I was curious about the people attending.
How can a college student afford the cost of watching a movie weekly?
Is the movie theater accessible enough for everyone?
Do people know what movie theaters are in their area?
Do they know of the deals going on each day?

Overall, I had about 4 pages of observations throughout a span of 4 days with 10 minutes each day.
I noted the date and time of each observation along with some sketching.
I wrote down everything I saw that ranged from what customers would do to the cleanliness of the theater itself.

After the observations, I completed two interviews according to my problem area regarding the movie theater.
Once the interviews were over, I completed my transcriptions and created a cultural probe.
The cultural probe consisted of a journal, a disposable camera, map, stickers, and pencils.
The results I got from the cultural probe were interesting to say the least.
I gathered the data about the person's lives, thoughts, and values, and kept those in the back of my mind.

INTO TEAMS Based on my observations, findings from the interviews, and cultural probe, I was then paired up with 5 other people.

My Role: Interviews, Storyboards, Personas, and collaborate on Affinity Diagram, Empathy Diagram, Requirements, Mockups, and Evaluations.

We decided to create a movie theater application; however, we mainly focused on the accessibility aspect.
We then worked together to create an affinity diagram.
It was grouped into several categories: Theater Experience, Handicap Accessibility, Home Theater Experience, and Watching Movies Online.
The conclusion was that people wish their theater-going experience could be better.
Whether it's ordering concessions or making movie-going more interactive, it could be improved.
For handicap accessibility, there could be an improved assisted listening system.
The system could also be more handicap-friendly because, sometimes, using it is difficult.
For home theater experience, it was found that too many people watch movies at home.
Sometimes it can be hard to watch a movie if it's on a smaller screen.
For watching movies online, improving access to movie information was the only complaint.
It was preferred to watch a movie online.

An empathy diagram was created afterward, and it was to determine why people have mixed feelings toward accessibility in theaters.
There was a general consensus that a lot of people feel sad or angry about accessibility and the rest tend to not say or do much.
From that information, the new question was: why would people decide to stay at home instead of watching physical movies in the theater?
Price and comfort is a huge factor, but the theater really should be more accessible to everyone.
Two personas were then created:

1. Jane Doe: A college student that attends the movie theater on a biweekly basis.
However, she hates enduring the wait times for the box office and concessions.
With that said, she would rather stay at home instead of driving to the theater.
However, she prefers to watch movies at the theater because of the big screen and surround system.
As a college student, she finds that the prices of the tickets and concessions are too much for her.

2. John Doe: A college student unable to go to the theater often due to his lack of free time.
If he didn't have to search different websites for information, he would be more interested in going out.
His goal is to maximize his own time and retrieve good results.
He enjoys watching Netflix and Amazon; however, since the movies are older, he is conflicted.

USER REQUIREMENTS Afterward, we performed a brainstorming session for the user requirements.
The goal was to understand what the application should be before creating paper prototypes.
There were three brainstorming session in total, and the solution ideas ranged from useful to wacky.
Some of the ideas were: complimentary blankets and glasses to see subtitles.
It was best to get all of our ideas on paper before beginning the mockups.

After creating the user requirements, it was decided that the 4 user needs are:
1. Usability: The application should be easy and familiar to the users.
2. Cross Platform: It should be usable on a computer, tablet, or phone.
3. Affordability: There will be no charge for the app, so it must be affordable to the developers.
4. Scalability: It should be easy to make changes and improvements.

DESIGN ALTERNATIVES 1. App that sorts the theater by price near your current location
This design was geared toward college students on a budget.
The top of the screen would show the date and movie.
After the location services are turned on, the app will determine which theaters are closest to them based on price.
It would then show the top three results, but the user can view more results if they desire.

2. App that provides movie information and recommendations from friends based on your movie interests.
This design was geared toward students who really care about recommendations before watching movies.
The top of the screen would be the user's profile, and the user would have to include what types of movies they like.
Their friends would then recommend them movies.
If the user clicks on their recommendation, it would take them to another screen about the movie's information.

3. App that allows users to see the seating options in the theater.
This would allow users to see which seats are open before navigating in the dark.
The top of the screen would show the movie theater, movie, time, and location of the movie they are watching.
When the app determines where they are, it will outline the theater's seating options.
Since safety is an issue, a ticket number would be required to use this application.

In the end, it was decided to put all three alternatives into one final application.
Our users should be presented with the cheapest options, be recommended movies from their friends, and have a pleasant seat-finding experience.

STORYBOARDS The next logical step was to create a couple storyboards to show how the application would be used on a day-to-day basis.

1. The first storyboard uses the persona, John Doe, to depict a scenario.
Since he prefers to stay at home, he uses the app to show him the cheapest and closest theater in his area.
That way, he won't have to waste extra time and can get back to his college work without having to drive far.

2. The second storyboard uses the persona, Jane Doe, to depict a scenario.
Since she is a college student on a budget, she uses the app to show her the prices of the movies.
She determines if the theater is on its discount day.
If it is, she goes to the movies and saves a couple extra dollars.

SUMMARY After converting the prototype from paper onto Photoshop, we performed a cognitive walkthrough of the design.
Based on those walkthroughs, it was discovered that there was crucial functionality missing from the design.
Users were unable to complete the task: registration and searching.
Users were unable to log in if they didn't already have an account, and they could not search for theaters in another city.

A think aloud evaluation was performed next.
We determined the pros and cons of the prototype by asking users for their suggestions on improvements.
Users believed that the overall design was implemented well, but the main problem was searching.

Lastly, a heuristic evaluation was performed.
It helped us analyze the different user interface problems that we should resolve.
The app did not return any error response to the user when they submitted an incorrect username or password.
The app did not include any documentation on how to use it.
The app also did not provide any system status updates.

Through the evaluations and the entire process, we understood that key functionality can easily slip past a design.
The evaluations helped us understand what our users want.

If we had more time on the project, we would include:
  • Search Function: For users to search up movies/theaters by name.
  • Registration: For new users to sign up.
  • Offline Use: For users that don't have access to the internet.

EEE SCOUT

April 6, 2016 - June 10, 2016
EEE Scout is an elicitation/specification project of HCI requirements in INF 132: Project in Human Computer Interaction by a group of 6 students.

THE PROBLEMS EEE Scout is a web app for building forms and accepting submissions of completed forms.
The goal of this project was to evaluate the usability and usefulness of EEE Scout.
It was designed specifically for the use of the UCI community.

In the customer's view, their problems were:
1. The site is used mostly for scholarship forms.
2. They would like tips on improving the site to increase usability.
3. They would like comprehensive tests to be conducted on creating forms and reviewing submission results.
4. They would like shorter tests on the completion of Scout forms.

Upon initial review, we were unable to find a lot wrong with the site, so we started user testing right away.
We observed 18 undergrads with varying levels of technical abilities and received 14 completed surveys.

For the most part, they all had the same problems/suggestions:
1. Icons: The hamburger icon was confusing. It was used as a way to grab and move a question.
2. Short Answer vs. Essay: There was no character count limit for text responses, so users didn't know when to use each option.
3. Multiple Choice vs. Multiple Select: The wording was confusing. Multiple Choice = One Answer Select, Multiple Select = Multiple Answers Allowed.
4. Container Truncates Text: It was found that the lowercase letters were being truncated.
5. Text Override: Typing in the header won't override the new sample application.
6. Done Button at Top: Some users found this button as counter-intuitive.
7. Link Opens New Tab: Users had issues navigating back to the page they wanted.

Users also requested certain features within the application:
1. Include Likert Scale Questions (Strongly Agree, Agree, Neutral, Disagree, Strongly Disagree)
2. Option of some questions being required: All questions are optional and users could submit forms without answering anything.
3. Customization: Users are accustomed to high levels of customization in other forms, so they wanted the same features.

METHODS 1. Primary Method: Interviews with the users who initially interacted with the site and other UCI students with opinions on Scout.
2. Secondary Method: Mix of User Testing and Questionnaires to understand their interactions with the product and their feelings/thoughts.

The user types were:
1. Entire UCI population: The entirety of UCI would use this product, and there would be a mix of users that vary from UCI Students to Professors.
2. Other Students: Porting Scout to other universities is a future possibility, so other students outside of UCI should be able to use the product as well.

The two main subgroups were:
1. Those who have used EEE Scout
2. Those who have not used EEE Scout

The task for the users:
Step 1: Have the user interact with the site and familiarize themselves.
Step 2: Have the users create their own forms given a certain scenario.
Step 3: Have the users fill out a feedback form on their experience.
It would ask who they were, the purpose of the form they created, if they've used other form systems, issues they ran into, and if they would use the site again.


USER TESTING/EVALUATIONS Overall, there were 6 participants available for in-person user testing.
They were simultaneously given the task to go onto EEE Scout and make a form.
They were encouraged to ask questions throughout the process and speak their mind.

A few statements were:
"It would be helpful if there was an undo button."
"Is there a way to limit word count?"
"There should be a help video that explains everything."
"I like the dragging and dropping."

Next, 3 out of 6 of the participants were available for an interview about the process afterward.
They were asked what purpose they create forms for, their general thoughts on the site, any problems, and any features they would like to see.
Generally, the participants used form builders for different things (if they were president of a club or for a class).
Google Forms was mainly used, and they found that using Google Forms was simple.
With their newfound opinion on EEE Scout, they found Scout was basic but a little confusing.
Regardless, they still thought it was pretty solid the way it was.


Next, I created 8 heuristic evaluations using Jakob Neilson's Famous Heuristics List.
For the most part, there wasn't a lot of high priority heuristics besides the hamburger menu.
An example of a heuristic that scored a severity of 1 was error prevention:
The website does not return an error message if the user does not answer a question within the form.
It was ranked as low priority because EEE Scout wanted the ability of all questions being optional.

FEEDBACK/THE END After all of that, we sent out a feedback survey to some previous EEE Scout users.
Over 20 invitations for the survey were sent out; however, only 10 replied:
2 faculty, 4 staff, 1 administrative employee, 1 4th year student, 1 PhD candidate, and 1 graduate student.

When asked if they liked EEE Scout, 9 said "yes" while only 1 said "no".
The "no" was because the user believed it could be used for multiple raters to submit blind scores.
When asked about their overall experience, 9 said "it was good" while one said "it created more work" for them.
When asked what they wished EEE Scout had, they all stated different general features mentioned already.
One interesting finding, however, was one person wanting EEE Scout and DUE information systems integrated together.

In the end, we recommended that EEE Scout adopt more features from Google Forms for ease of use.
1. Allowing some customization is an attractive element.
2. Introducing question logic will allow flexibility in creating forms.
3. Providing the option for anonymous submissions could entice more users.
Besides the minor details mentioned earlier, the site is great and useful for the purpose it was built.
The site is intuitive and delivered positive feedback.
The only issues found were minor ones, and the major problems could easily be fixed.
At the end of the day, the client was generous in sharing their vision for EEE Scout with the team throughout this study.
